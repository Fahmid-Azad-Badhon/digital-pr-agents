When Bad Wipers Equal Deadly Driving
Study data suggests that roughly 46 million U.S. people drive with impaired vision in bad weather due to poor windshield wiper performance. Bad windshield wipers significantly impair visibility, and contribute to nearly 1.2 million crashes and over 6,000 fatalities every year in the U.S. Yet 24 states – including Florida – currently feature no vehicle safety inspection requirements, exacerbating the risk factor.

This study will consider how having bad windshield wipers can equal deadly consequences. We’ll look at wiper maintenance data and fatality data that cites poor wipers as the determining factor. We’ll also focus on state differentials and how wiper maintenance obligations vary from state to state. And we’ll consider the parts of the U.S. that feature the kind of weather that means good windshield wipers are a must.

First, let’s look at some key windshield wiper facts.

The Safety Importance of Windshield Wipers: Key Data
Study data tells us that around 90% of driving decisions primarily rely on vision, with poor visibility linked to nearly 50% of fatal crashes.

Here are some worrying wiper-related statistics.

A survey of vehicles going through check lanes during National Car Care Month revealed that one in five vehicles had faulty windshield wipers or washing systems, potentially impairing the driver’s vision.
Roughly one in five of the 231,289,998 vehicles registered in the U.S. in 2004 (46,277,999) had deficient windshield wipers or washing systems, meaning tens of millions of drivers were hampered by impaired vision during stormy weather.
According to the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA), over 20% of all vehicle crashes are due to adverse weather conditions. 
Drivers often wait years to replace windshield wipers that were clearly faulty in some way after only six to 12 months.
According to The Bass Tire Company, wiper blades should be replaced every 6-12 months (or even sooner if you notice skipping, streaking, or torn rubber on your wiper blades).
It can’t be stressed too strenuously: wiper blades can save your life if it rains, snows, or hails, or if a vehicle up ahead kicking up dust or debris occludes your view. So making sure yours are in tip-top condition is vital.

Study data suggests that states featuring mandatory inspections show a comparative 5-9% reduction in crash rates. And it’s not an expensive habit: the average annual cost of a U.S. vehicle inspection is between $20–$70.

In terms of improving vehicle safety overall, the data tells its own story: the highest ranking state with no inspection requirements (South Carolina) has a fatality rate of 24.9 deaths per 100,000 people. To compare, California, which features routine inspections and is ranked 18th on the overall list, has a death rate of 10.9 per 100,000 people.





South Carolina, which ranks top overall with a death rate of 24.9 per 100k (more than double the national average rate of 12.2) and a rate of 1.79 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled (the national average is 1.26), features no safety inspection requirements. Clearly, the state might well benefit from a policy change regarding its car safety position: even minor vehicle safety obligations could save a significant number of lives. 

The same might be said for Arkansas, Alabama, Tennessee, and New Hampshire. All are ranked in the top 10 for fatalities by population, and none feature vehicle safety inspection requirements.

Maryland, which ranks 3rd on the overall list, requires a single vehicle inspection. Its fatality rate (by 100k of the population) of 20.7 suggests a more demanding safety protocol might be overdue. 

Ultimately, 11 states do not require any vehicle safety checks, and these states also feature a death rate higher than the national average. In all cases, it would seem worthwhile to impose some kind of mandatory inspection.

It’s also worth bearing in mind the states that suffer the nation’s worst weather when we consider vehicle safety inspections, since the vehicles in question desperately need fully-working windshield wiper blades.
